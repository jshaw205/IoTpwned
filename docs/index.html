Why it matters

Is your baby monitor letting strangers watch back?

Cheap cameras, doorbells, and routers ship with forgotten default passwords and services switched on by default. Botnets scan the whole internet for them around the clock. Most people never find out they're exposed — until IoTpwned tells them, in words that actually make sense, and exactly what to change.

What it checks

Seven checks, one plain-English grade.

Every finding comes with why it matters and how to fix it — no jargon dump, no scare tactics.

▸ Exposed devices & ports local

Finds every device on your network and the risky services they're running — Telnet, VNC, RDP, exposed camera and router admin panels.

▸ Device fingerprinting local

Labels what each device actually is — "Hikvision camera", "TP-Link router" — from its network fingerprint, so the advice is specific.

▸ Known vulnerabilities + opt-in online

Matches your devices against a built-in list of notorious router/camera CVEs, offline. Opt in to also query the live NIST database.

▸ Wi-Fi encryption local

Reads your current Wi-Fi and flags weak encryption (Open, WEP, old WPA), nudging you toward WPA2/WPA3.

▸ Internet exposure opt-in

Checks what's reachable from the outside on your public IP via Shodan — the port-forward you forgot you set up.

▸ Default passwords opt-in

Actively tries well-known default logins (admin/admin and friends) on admin panels — the one check that logs in, and only on devices you own.

▸ An A–F network grade local

Everything rolls up into one score you can act on — and re-run after you've made changes to watch it climb.

How it works

From download to graded in under a minute.

STEP 1

Run it

Download the one-file app and open it, or pip install it. No dependencies, no sign-up.

STEP 2

Confirm & scan

Tick "I own this network", press Scan. By default it only checks what's open — it doesn't try to log in.

STEP 3

Get your grade

Read your A–F report card in the terminal or your browser, and work down the fixes.

Privacy-first, by design

Nothing leaves your machine.

It's a security tool — so it holds itself to the standard it checks for. The scan is entirely local.

  • ✓ No cloud, no account, no telemetry — the code is open source.
  • ✓ It never tries a password unless you turn on the optional default-password check — off by default, and it asks first.
  • ✓ The two online checks are strictly opt-in, ask first, and send only a device brand name or your public IP — never your data.
